Granny

The fans rattled as they moved hot air through the warehouse. Packed with spectators, sharp whistles bounced off the walls, slicing through the humidity of the tournament. The blue mat stuck to the bottoms of my feet, as I squared up for the sparring match. My eyes wandered away from my opponent, though, and found Granny’s platinum blonde bob. My stomach twisted. She was sitting next to my dad, arms crossed, staring down at me through a pair of green and black sunglasses. It was the championship match. My opponent was nine, two years older than me. We were dressed in white doboks and padding with red targets on our chests. She bobbed up and down on her toes, while my coach gently reminded me to put my hands up to protect myself. I chewed on my mouthguard as my eyes moved between my opponent and Granny. The referee signaled for us to begin. The girl let out a war-cry that rattled my bones. She darted across the mat and reared a decapitating roundhouse. I dodged. I drove an open palm onto the red dot on her chest. She shouted, stumbled, so I did it again. She traded blows with me. I felt the sting of copper in my mouth. I hit her even harder. Each time I punched she stumbled backward. I slammed my palms on the red dot over and over until the wind was knocked out of her. I didn’t notice if she was fighting back anymore. My frenzy blocked it out, and only stopped when I heard the whistle. My opponent had stepped off the mat. I’d won. It was the first time I had won anything. I looked to the crowd to see my dad cheering and clapping. Granny was stagnant, looking down on me. Granny brought a digital camera with her everywhere she went. When I was awarded my medal, she rapid-fired photos with the flash on. She admired them, saying how much I looked like my father, making me cringe. But I didn’t dare show it. I had a vision that if I did, it would end with me digging my own grave on a patch of red dirt in the parking lot. After Granny had my dad take a photo of the two of us, she gripped my shoulder with an iron fist and pulled me aside next to a fan. One of its blades was banging against the cage around it. The breeze dried the sweat on my skin, making me shiver. She towered above me as she said, “that wasn’t 110 percent, Miss Emma.” “110 percent?” “You didn’t give 110 percent of your effort.” “But I won.” “And?” I held up the gold medal as a response, and repeated, “I won.” “That wasn’t the best you could do.” My heart stung. The feeling festered. I learned the meaning of hierarchy from Granny: Granny sits at the peak of a mountain where the air is thin, but she doesn’t feel the difference. I can’t see her from the bottom; she’s obscured by a blizzard. She stayed away from my line of sight. But I felt Granny’s presence watching me, her legacy, compete with her expectations. Growing up, I found myself slipping away during family events at her home. The rooms were like a museum draped in stretches of black and white lining, dark masonry, and emerald ceilings. There was an exception though. One wing held a hallway that was decorated with framed newspaper clippings of her and her husband’s family business. Her face was plastered underneath headlines all written along the same lines, but with different dates: Most influential, charitable, entrepreneur in Oklahoma. At the end of this hallway was her living room. Lighting the room was a wall of stained-glass windows that cast green, gold, and red light onto more photographs. The photographs packed all the walls from the floor to the ceiling. They ranged from black and white, grainy colored photos, and sharp saturated ones. I saw photos of her and Grandad from when they were young, when she had jet black hair and he was still a cherry blond. The centerpiece of the room was a massive painting of her grandchildren. She’s never taken a photo down, only added. I took notes of her clothes, her home, her sons and daughters, remarks about her past, and every word she’s ever said to me outside of ‘I miss you.’ All I wanted to know was her, who she really was, instead of the silhouette of a person I saw looming above me. This mosaic of notes will paint a rough, incomplete, portrait of her. But there are a few things I know for certain about her: Granny is eighty-four and has not retired. She will never retire, not even in her grave. She spends her time aiding various charities, tending to her nine grandchildren and business, and attending pickle-ball lessons with her friends. She will pay close attention to every habit, tic, and expression someone unknowingly gives her. She can uncover any hidden desire, or intention. In her old age, she uses it to work a room in her favor. I’ve seen her do it at holiday parties, cocktail hours, and weddings. She’d make some time to say hello and snap a photo of me, before she found her targets: the Tulsa oil tycoons, their real estate developer juniors, and the nouveau riche of the Great Plains. Granny would make the rounds; she’d greet each man with a smile. The handshake involved her allowing them to clasp her hand, but she would be the one to place her hand on their forearm. Sometimes it was a man with old money in his pocket, other times it could’ve been a lucky investment in Apple in ’78. To Granny, they just had to be rich. Not even handsome. Little did these men know, her emerald acrylics were being used to hook them in. The hooks were hidden by her asking about their grandchildren, or how they were holding up with the loss of their alma mater football team. As the conversation floated between niceties and some gossip, she would yank on the hook, sinking it in. “I was just talking to Jake Foster over there, and he was telling me about how he heard that Gerald, junior not senior, decided that he’s going to donate 200k to Catholic Charities. I said, only 200k!” She joked. The man gave her a tight-lipped smile. “Are y’all close to your goal?” “Well, I pledged about 350k, so now we’re at about two million.” Granny wouldn’t break her gaze from the man as she said this. She would keep her shoulders square to him. She knew that he had made three times more than her in that year. The man would continue the conversation as normal, his mind spinning as he felt the room closing in on him. How could Jake Foster, Gerald Junior, and a woman each be donating more than him? With a sip of her glass, followed by an email from the rich man to his secretary after the cocktails were dried up, a woman’s hospital could be built. Next, it would be a school for homeless children. After that, a rehab center for the opioid epidemic. She could fundraise millions of dollars in seven minutes, by measuring dicks. None of those facilities would exist without her methodology. I can only imagine what she used to use it for when she and her husband were first building the business. My father told me she was the one who balanced the books. She had dropped her studies in journalism to help my grandfather, a dropout too, in a forgotten town that had no sidewalks, flooded when it rained an inch, and their business was the only gas station for miles. The station was nestled between a gristmill, the industrial jewel of the town, and a dilapidated motel. Granny’s role was the rake through the nitty gritties of the operation for any hidden oversights. She also tended to a growing family, while Grandad would expand the business according to her calculations. During this time of her life, I imagine her sitting at the base of a mountain, searching for possible routes up as she clawed through numbers and inventory. The frigid wind was harsh against her black hair, not yet bleached blonde. She found her foothold and climbed alone. I wonder what kept her climbing, how did she not fall with her struggles and the responsibilities of the business and her children? What fueled her to keep working? And why have inherited her compulsion to climb alone? Once, at eighteen, I was at her home for a birthday party. It was spring. The room warmed with a swell of loud voices. I looked across the room, past the crowd of family members, and watched Granny pour a heavy glass of red wine in her kitchen. The green and gold light illuminated her outline and turned the wine bright red as she took the first sip. She made the rounds afterward, sitting down and speaking to each child and grandchild until their turn was up. Granny walked as if she were floating, with her shoulders back, and when she spoke to people, she would stand too close. Her hand rested on her hip as she sipped and gave them a practiced smile. Granny set her glass on the coaster next to me. Her posture was perfect as she sat in the lazy boy. She wore white jeans, sandals, and a white blouse. At seven he thought the garden was too muddy, so he busted out her new vacuum and cleaned up the mulch and flowers himself. He graduated high school a few months later and shipped off to play football out of state; he spent two years there before flunking out and moving back to Oklahoma. She told him this at a dive bar, the type that had sticky wooden floors and a flickering, blue, Coors Light sign in the window. He’d met her three years earlier. She appeared to Frank in fragments, one moment he glimpsed her wavy, brunette, hair bouncing as she passed him on the sidewalk, and the next he saw her siting in the park with her nose in an anatomy textbook.
